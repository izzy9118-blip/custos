STRAUSS'S TAXONOMY OF LITERARY CONCEALMENT

CURRENT SOURCE RANGE: pp. 70–77

PURPOSE

This Hermeneutic Object records, classifies, and preserves every literary technique by which concealment is accomplished that Leo Strauss explicitly names, enumerates, distinguishes, or reconstructs.

The object is cumulative. It must be extended whenever continued reading reveals an additional named literary device, concealment mechanism, hint, compositional operation, or rule for distinguishing concealed teaching from surface teaching.

No technique may be silently merged with another. Strauss's distinctions must be preserved even where two devices appear functionally related.

DOCUMENTARY CORRECTION

The six explicit methods of hiding contradictions occur on pp. 70–72, not pp. 88–89. Additional concealment devices and hints follow on pp. 74–77. The earlier location note attached to DF-000129 is therefore superseded for source-location purposes but remains part of the historical record.

I. GOVERNING DOCUMENTARY PRINCIPLE

Strauss states:

"Since the contradictions in the Guide are concealed, we must briefly consider at least some of the ways of hiding contradictions."

This sentence establishes that concealment is not treated merely as a general intention. Strauss reconstructs specific literary techniques by which concealment is accomplished.

II. EXPLICITLY ENUMERATED METHODS OF HIDING CONTRADICTIONS

TECHNIQUE LC-001
NAME: Separation by Distant Placement
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "to speak of the same subject in a contradictory manner on pages far apart from each other"
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 70
MECHANISM:
Two contradictory statements concerning the same subject are separated by substantial textual distance. Ordinary reading fails to hold both statements together, allowing the contradiction to remain concealed.
STRAUSS'S SYMBOL:
a = b (page 15) — a ≠ b (page 379)
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The distance between the statements prevents casual recognition of their incompatibility.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
The reader must compare statements across the whole work rather than relying on local context alone.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

TECHNIQUE LC-002
NAME: Incidental Placement of One Contradictory Statement
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "to make one of the two contradictory statements in passing, as it were"
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 70
MECHANISM:
One statement is prominently presented while its contradiction appears incidentally, parenthetically, or as an apparently subordinate remark.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The unequal prominence of the two statements causes the incidental statement to escape notice or to be denied equal interpretive weight.
EXAMPLE IDENTIFIED BY STRAUSS:
Maimonides' incidental denial of the obligatory character of the entire sacrificial legislation.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Incidental remarks must be indexed and compared with prominent declarations concerning the same subject.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

TECHNIQUE LC-003
NAME: Contradiction of Implications Rather Than Direct Statement
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "to contradict the first statement not directly, but by contradicting its implications"
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 70–71
MECHANISM:
A statement is not directly denied. Instead, another statement denies a proposition that follows from it. The contradiction becomes visible only after the unstated implications are reconstructed.
STRAUSS'S SYMBOL:
a = b — b = c — [a = c] — a ≠ c — [a ≠ b]
The bracketed propositions are not pronounced.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The contradiction remains concealed because the incompatible propositions are never placed in direct verbal opposition.
EXAMPLES IDENTIFIED BY STRAUSS:
1. "One of the main subjects of the Guide is ma'aseh bereshit."
2. "Ma'aseh bereshit is physics."
3. "Physics is not a subject of the Guide."

A second example contrasts:
1. "Explanation of the secrets is impossible by nature."
2. "Explanation of the secrets is forbidden by the law."
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
The reader must reconstruct logical implications and compare them with later statements.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

TECHNIQUE LC-004
NAME: Apparent Repetition with Minute Addition or Omission
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "to contradict the first statement not directly, but by seemingly repeating it while actually adding to it, or omitting from it, an apparently negligible expression"
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 71
MECHANISM:
A later statement appears to repeat an earlier statement but changes it by adding or omitting a small expression. The minute verbal difference produces a substantive contradiction.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The appearance of repetition conceals the change. The reader's expectation of sameness prevents close examination of the altered wording.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Parallel passages must be compared word by word. Additions and omissions, however small, must be recorded.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

TECHNIQUE LC-005
NAME: Intermediary Assertion and Creeping Contradiction
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "to introduce between the two contradictory statements an intermediary assertion"
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 71
MECHANISM:
An intermediary statement is introduced. By itself, it does not contradict the first statement. Through the addition or omission of an apparently negligible expression, however, the intermediary statement is later repeated in a form that contradicts the first. Strauss says that "the contradictory statement creeps in as a repetition of the intermediary statement."
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The intermediary assertion masks the transition from the first position to its contradiction. The final statement appears continuous with what precedes it.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
The reader must reconstruct the sequence of formulations, not merely compare the first and final statements.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

TECHNIQUE LC-006
NAME: Use of Ambiguous Words
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION: "To use ambiguous words."
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 71–72
MECHANISM:
A word carries more than one meaning. One meaning supports the exterior or conventional teaching; another supports the interior or concealed teaching.
EXAMPLE IDENTIFIED BY STRAUSS:
The statement "a certain statement is an addition" may mean either:
1. a true addition to an untruth; or
2. an untrue addition to the truth.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The same verbal surface communicates differently to different readers.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Words must be examined for both technical and nontechnical meanings, as well as for exterior and interior senses.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND ENUMERATED

III. STRAUSS'S FURTHER ANALYSIS OF AMBIGUITY

TECHNIQUE LC-007
NAME: Two-Faced Speech
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 72
STRAUSS'S DESCRIPTION:
A speech may have "two faces," an exterior face useful for social or political purposes and an inner face useful for knowledge of the truth.
MECHANISM:
The same speech is directed toward two audiences. The vulgar receive the exterior meaning; the independent reader may recover the inner meaning.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Audience differentiation permits simultaneous revelation and concealment.
RELATIONSHIP:
This is the broader communicative structure within which LC-006 operates.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY DISTINGUISHED

TECHNIQUE LC-008
NAME: Concealment in a Single Common Word
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 72
STRAUSS'S DESCRIPTION:
A secret is more perfectly concealed in a word than in a sentence because the word is smaller and therefore a better hiding place. Common words placed unobtrusively within unobtrusive sentences are especially effective.
MECHANISM:
A decisive hidden meaning is concentrated in an ordinary word that does not attract attention.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The ordinary appearance of the word prevents the reader from recognizing its doctrinal force.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Every word must be scrutinized, especially common words that appear incidental.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY RECONSTRUCTED

TECHNIQUE LC-009
NAME: Secret Terminology
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 74–75
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
"We may call those words secret words. His secret terminology requires a special study."
MECHANISM:
A recurring vocabulary contains words that possess, or may possess, concealed meanings. Some are ambiguous; others are unambiguous but technically or contextually coded.
EXAMPLES LISTED BY STRAUSS:
"the wise"; "the learned"; "the men of speculation"; "the virtuous"; "the community of the believers in [God's] unity"; "government"; "providence"; "addition"; "secret"; "belief"; "action"; "possible"; and the unambiguous terms adamiyyun, fiqh, and dunya.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
A controlled vocabulary allows concealed teaching to recur without explicit disclosure.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Construct a complete index of words that have or may have secret meaning.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED

IV. OTHER DEVICES NAMED BY STRAUSS

TECHNIQUE LC-010
NAME: Intentional Sophism
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
Strauss refers to "intentional sophisms" as devices used by Maimonides, though he does not elaborate them in this immediate passage.
MECHANISM:
A deliberately fallacious or misleading argument may protect or obscure the actual teaching.
DOCUMENTARY LIMIT:
The present passage names but does not classify subtypes.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED — REQUIRES FURTHER DOCUMENTARY DEVELOPMENT

TECHNIQUE LC-011
NAME: Ironical Remark
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
Strauss names "ironical remarks" among Maimonides' devices without elaborating their varieties here.
MECHANISM:
The apparent meaning of a statement differs from the meaning intended for the attentive reader.
DOCUMENTARY LIMIT:
The present passage names the device but does not provide a complete operational account.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED — REQUIRES FURTHER DOCUMENTARY DEVELOPMENT

TECHNIQUE LC-012
NAME: Apostrophe to the Reader
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 75
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
"various kinds of apostrophes to the reader"
MECHANISM:
Direct addresses to the reader may signal audience, level of disclosure, warning, invitation, or restriction.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The form and placement of direct address may distinguish intended readers or mark concealed instruction.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED — SUBTYPES NOT YET RECONSTRUCTED

TECHNIQUE LC-013
NAME: Mottoes
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 75
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
"mottoes prefixed to the whole work or to individual parts"
MECHANISM:
A prefatory saying or scriptural phrase supplies an indirect interpretive key to the work or section it precedes.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The motto communicates through allusion rather than direct exposition.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED — EXAMPLES REQUIRE CONTINUED COLLECTION

TECHNIQUE LC-014
NAME: Silence or Significant Omission
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 75–77
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
"Another device consists in silence, i.e., the omission of something which only the learned, or the learned who are able to understand of themselves, would miss."
MECHANISM:
Expected information is withheld. Only readers possessing the relevant knowledge recognize that something is absent.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The omission differentiates readers while signaling the exoteric character of the surrounding passage.
EXAMPLES IDENTIFIED BY STRAUSS:
1. Maimonides' incomplete quotation of Aristotle concerning the sense of touch.
2. Omission of two words that would identify Aristotle's statement as common opinion.
3. Failure to mention immortality of the soul or resurrection of the body in the explicit discussion of divine providence.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Recover the expected source or doctrinal context and document what is missing.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND EXEMPLIFIED

TECHNIQUE LC-015
NAME: Deliberate Misquotation Through Omission
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 75–76
MECHANISM:
A cited statement omits words that would substantially alter its meaning or disclose its status.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
The altered quotation supports an exterior teaching while allowing knowledgeable readers to recover the concealed qualification.
RELATIONSHIP:
A specific subtype of LC-014, but retained separately because Strauss's example identifies citation alteration as a distinct documentary operation.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY RECONSTRUCTED

TECHNIQUE LC-016
NAME: Repetition as Leitmotif
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 75
STRAUSS'S DESCRIPTION:
Fourfold repetition of an express quotation in a carefully worded book proves that the quotation is "something like a leitmotif."
MECHANISM:
Repeated language marks a theme for special attention while its altered or incomplete form may conceal its true significance.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Repetition signals importance, but must be examined together with exact wording and source recovery.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY RECONSTRUCTED

TECHNIQUE LC-017
NAME: Rashei Perakim — Beginnings of Chapters
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 77
STRAUSS'S FORMULATION:
The expression may mean not only "chapter headings" but also "beginnings of chapters." Maimonides sometimes gives important hints through the initial word or words of a chapter.
MECHANISM:
A chapter's opening word or phrase indirectly signals the subject, division, or doctrinal relation of the section.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Initial placement gives a small lexical item architectonic force.
INVESTIGATIVE REQUIREMENT:
Record the first word or words of every chapter and test them against the structure of the work.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AND EXEMPLIFIED

V. SUPPORTING CONCEALMENT OPERATIONS NAMED BY STRAUSS

TECHNIQUE LC-018
NAME: Rare Statement of the Unconventional Teaching
SOURCE LOCATION: pp. 73–74
MECHANISM:
The conventional view is repeated frequently, while the unconventional or true view is stated rarely, perhaps only once.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Frequency protects the conventional teaching; rarity conceals the truth from inattentive readers.
STRAUSS'S RULE:
Of two contradictory statements, the statement occurring least frequently is presumptively the one Maimonides considered true.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY FORMULATED RULE OF DISCERNMENT

TECHNIQUE LC-019
NAME: Frequent Repetition of the Conventional View
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
MECHANISM:
Views suitable to or accepted by the vulgar are repeated as frequently as possible.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Repetition gives the surface teaching apparent authority and familiarity while covering the rarer contrary statement.
RELATIONSHIP:
Operates jointly with LC-018.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY RECONSTRUCTED

TECHNIQUE LC-020
NAME: Hint
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
MECHANISM:
A small textual indication assists the attentive reader in discovering a contradiction or determining which contradictory statement is true.
DOCUMENTARY FUNCTION:
Hints guide readers capable of understanding by themselves without directly disclosing the teaching.
STRAUSS'S DISTINCTION:
Recognizing hints requires a higher degree of independent understanding than recognizing an obvious contradiction.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED — SUBTYPES PARTLY IDENTIFIED THROUGH OTHER DEVICES

TECHNIQUE LC-021
NAME: Inappropriate Expression
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
MECHANISM:
A seemingly ill-fitting expression may function as a stumbling block that directs attention beneath the surface.
DOCUMENTARY LIMIT:
Strauss notes that it can also be dismissed as merely inappropriate; therefore it does not compel inquiry as strongly as a contradiction.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AS A POSSIBLE DEVICE

TECHNIQUE LC-022
NAME: Clumsy Transition
SOURCE LOCATION: p. 74
MECHANISM:
An awkward movement between subjects may signal a concealed relationship or structural break.
DOCUMENTARY LIMIT:
Like an inappropriate expression, it may be dismissed as mere clumsiness unless corroborated.
STATUS: EXPLICITLY NAMED AS A POSSIBLE DEVICE

VII. GOVERNING REPOSITORY RULE

Whenever Strauss names, classifies, enumerates, distinguishes, or exemplifies a literary technique of concealment, the investigator must:

1. Record the technique in this Hermeneutic Object.
2. Preserve Strauss's own terminology.
3. Record the exact page number.
4. Preserve the primary passage.
5. Describe the documentary mechanism without exceeding the evidence.
6. Record every example Strauss supplies.
7. Distinguish explicitly named techniques from repository-level reconstructions.
8. Preserve uncertainty where Strauss names a device without fully explaining it.
9. Never merge techniques silently.
10. Advance the version number whenever the object is materially extended.
