## Shared Examination Packet

### Strauss's opening problem

Strauss begins Chapter I by saying that Machiavelli presented his political
teaching in two books whose relation is obscure. He then observes:

> “At the beginning of the Prince, Machiavelli divides all states into two
> classes, republics and principalities.”

He adds:

> “Above all, Machiavelli says explicitly that in the Prince he will deal
> solely with principalities and will not discuss republics there since he has
> done so elsewhere at length.”

Strauss's complete note is:

> “1. Prince chs. 1 beginning, 2 beginning and 8 beginning.”

Immediately afterward, Strauss identifies the unnamed work on republics with
the *Discourses*, then complicates the apparent division by observing that the
*Discourses* also treats kingdoms and that *The Prince* contains many
references to republics. The two books' relation therefore remains obscure.

### Passages Strauss asks the reader to follow

*Prince*, chapter 1, beginning, normalized from the fixed Ricci/Vincent OCR:

> “All states and dominions which hold or have held sway over mankind are
> either republics or monarchies.”

*Prince*, chapter 2, beginning, normalized from the fixed Ricci/Vincent OCR:

> “I will not here speak of republics, having already treated of them fully in
> another place. I will deal only with monarchies...”

*Prince*, chapter 8, beginning, normalized from the fixed Ricci/Vincent OCR:

> “But as there are still two ways of becoming prince which cannot be
> attributed entirely either to fortune or to ability, they must not be passed
> over, although one of them could be more fully discussed if we were treating
> of republics.”

Material limit: these English passages are normalized from a visibly corrupted
OCR witness. They support shared reading at the derived-witness level; exact
Italian wording and variants remain unresolved.

### Open questions

1. Before following the note, what does Strauss's phrase “Above all” make the
   three citations appear to prove about the relation of *The Prince* and the
   *Discourses*?
2. Why does Strauss cite chapter 8 as well as the much more obvious beginnings
   of chapters 1 and 2?
3. Does chapter 8 merely preserve the republic/principality boundary, or does
   its republican treatment of a way of “becoming prince” complicate that
   boundary?
4. How is the reader's understanding changed when Strauss first uses the note
   to support a clean division and then, in the surrounding pages, shows that
   neither book stays within it?
